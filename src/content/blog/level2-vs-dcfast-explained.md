# Level 2 vs DC Fast Charging: Which Is Right for Your Property?

EV charging hardware is not one-size-fits-all. The technology that works at a highway rest stop is not the right technology for an apartment complex, and installing the wrong type creates an expensive mismatch between what your users need and what the infrastructure delivers.

This guide explains the practical difference between Level 2 and DC fast charging, and how to match the right technology to your property and use case.

## The Core Difference: Speed vs. Dwell Time

The fundamental distinction between Level 2 and DC fast charging is speed — and speed matters only in the context of how long a driver will be at your property.

**Level 2 (AC) charging** operates at 7.2–19.2 kW and adds roughly 15–30 miles of range per hour. A full charge from near-empty takes 6–12 hours, depending on the vehicle and charger output. Level 2 is the standard for home charging and is well-suited to locations where vehicles are parked for extended periods.

**DC Fast Charging (DCFC)** delivers 50–300 kW directly to the battery, bypassing the vehicle's onboard converter. At 150–180kW, a DCFC can add 100+ miles of range in 15–20 minutes and bring many EVs from 20% to 80% in under 30 minutes. It's designed for high-turnover locations where drivers need significant range added quickly.

The practical implication: if your users will be parked for hours, Level 2 is sufficient and far more cost-effective to install. If your users stop briefly and need fast range restoration, DCFC is appropriate.

## Where Level 2 Makes Sense

**Multifamily residential** is the strongest use case for Level 2. Tenants park overnight — typically 8–12 hours. That's more than enough time to fully charge any current EV on a Level 2 connection. Installing DC fast chargers in a residential garage would be like putting a fire hose in a kitchen: more power than the use case requires, at significantly higher cost.

Evium's Level 2 lineup includes the EVOCHARGE 32A and 40A wall-mounted units and the MaxiCharger AC Elite Business pedestal for commercial settings. These handle residential and light commercial applications reliably.

**Commercial and office** properties are also strong Level 2 candidates. An employee parking for a 7–9 hour workday gets a full charge, the same as overnight residential. The charging is invisible to the user — they arrive, plug in, and come back to a full battery with no session management required.

**Medical facilities** — hospitals, nursing homes, clinics — share the dwell-time profile of office properties. Staff and long-visit patients park for 4–12 hours. Level 2 handles that use case cleanly.

**Retail** depends on dwell time. A grocery store where the average visit is 30–45 minutes is not a strong Level 2 use case — not enough time to add meaningful range. A large format retail destination, shopping center, or mixed-use property where people spend 2–4 hours is a better fit. Understanding your typical customer dwell time is the determining factor.

## Where DC Fast Makes Sense

**Highway and corridor locations** are the clearest DCFC use case. Drivers on long trips stop specifically to add range and continue their journey. Session duration is deliberate and short. High throughput is the goal. This is the NEVI corridor model, and it's where DC fast charging economics work best.

**High-traffic parking locations** with short average stays — urban parking garages, transit park-and-ride facilities, airport short-term lots — can benefit from DCFC when EV volume is high enough to justify the capital cost.

**Fleet depots** with scheduled turnaround requirements sometimes need DCFC, particularly for delivery vehicles that must be recharged and redeployed within a few hours. The faster the turnaround requirement, the stronger the case for DC fast.

## What About a Mix?

Many properties benefit from a combination. A mixed-use development with residential units above ground-floor retail might install Level 2 for resident parking and a DCFC or two for customer-facing spaces. A large commercial campus might anchor employee parking with Level 2 and add one fast charger for visitors.

The right mix depends on your user population, typical dwell times, and the number of EVs you expect to serve. Evium's initial site assessment covers this evaluation — analyzing the property, expected utilization patterns, and the optimal hardware configuration before any commitment is made.

## The Cost Consideration

DC fast chargers cost significantly more than Level 2 — both in hardware and installation. A single DCFC installation can run $50,000–$150,000+ depending on the charger power level and electrical work required. Level 2 installations are a fraction of that cost.

Under Evium's no-cost model, this doesn't directly affect the property owner's capital exposure. But it does affect the conversation about which product makes sense: installing a $100,000 DCFC at a location with 4-hour average dwell times is inefficient for everyone. The right charger is the one matched to the use case — and that matching is where the real value of an experienced partner shows up.

Getting the technology right from the start avoids expensive retrofits and ensures the infrastructure serves your users and generates revenue effectively from day one.
