# EV Charging Infrastructure: What to Expect in the Next Five Years

The EV charging landscape in five years will look substantially different from today — more chargers, better software, tighter grid integration, and a user experience that has matured significantly from the early patchwork of incompatible networks and unreliable hardware.

For property owners and operators making infrastructure decisions now, understanding where things are heading is useful context. Here's a grounded view of what the next five years look like.

## National Coverage Will Close In

The single biggest shift coming is scale. NEVI's $5 billion buildout is actively deploying corridor charging across every state. State and utility programs are layering on top. Private investment is accelerating.

The result is a national charging network that will approach the density and reliability of gasoline infrastructure within this decade. Long-distance EV travel will become routine and unplanned — drivers will charge when convenient rather than when necessary. That changes the experience of EV ownership at a population level, which accelerates adoption, which increases demand for charging at every property type.

## Smart Charging Will Become Standard

Today's chargers are increasingly networked. Tomorrow's will be smarter about when and how they deliver power.

Smart charging uses software to shift charging loads in response to grid signals, time-of-use pricing, or local capacity constraints. A multifamily property with 50 overnight chargers could have all of them draw power simultaneously during cheap off-peak hours and taper down during peak-demand windows — reducing the property's energy costs and participating in utility demand response programs.

OCPP and OpenADR (Open Automated Demand Response) are the protocols enabling this. Properties deploying charging infrastructure that supports both standards today are positioned to benefit from smart charging programs as they mature.

## Grid Integration Will Become a Revenue Source

As V2G (vehicle-to-grid) technology matures and regulatory frameworks develop, parked EV batteries will participate in grid services. A property with 20 EVs plugged in overnight has a meaningful amount of distributed storage — enough to matter for grid balancing, demand response, and potentially ancillary services markets.

The economics of this are still developing, but the direction is clear: charging infrastructure that can communicate bidirectionally with the grid will have additional revenue potential beyond session charging. Properties with the right hardware and software foundation will be positioned to participate.

## The User Experience Is Maturing

Early EV charging suffered from inconsistent uptime, fragmented payment systems, and poor driver interfaces. That's improving rapidly. Plug-and-charge — where the vehicle authenticates automatically without an app or card — is being rolled out. Payment systems are converging. Reliable uptime is increasingly expected, not hoped for.

As the user experience improves, utilization increases. Drivers who've been burned by dead chargers in the past become regular users when reliability is established. Properties with well-maintained, well-managed charging networks benefit from that behavioral shift.

## What This Means for Decisions Today

Infrastructure installed today will operate well into this five-year transformation. The decisions that hold up across it:

- OCPP-compliant hardware that can integrate with evolving software platforms
- Power levels appropriate for the use case with room to grow
- A management partner with the operational capability to keep utilization high as the network expands around it

The next five years will bring more competition for EV drivers' charging sessions, but also more EV drivers than today. Properties that build the infrastructure now — before the competition is fully established — have the advantage of serving a growing user base from a position of early presence.

The buildout is happening. The question is whether your property is part of it.
