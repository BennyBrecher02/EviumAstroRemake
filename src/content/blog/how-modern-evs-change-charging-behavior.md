# How Modern EV Ranges Are Changing When and How Drivers Charge

When the first mass-market EVs launched, range was the defining constraint. Drivers with 80–100 miles of range charged frequently, charged opportunistically, and planned routes around charging locations. A short workday charge topped up a meaningful percentage of the battery. Every available charger mattered.

That dynamic is changing. Current-generation EVs routinely offer 250–350+ miles of range, and battery technology continues to improve. The behavioral implications for charging infrastructure — particularly for property owners deciding what to install and where — are meaningful.

## Longer Range Means Less Frequent Charging

A driver with 300 miles of range who commutes 40 miles per day might only need to charge once or twice a week. Compare that to a driver with 100 miles of range on the same commute — they're charging nearly every day.

This changes utilization patterns in several ways:

**Charging becomes more intentional.** Rather than topping off whenever a charger is convenient, drivers with long-range vehicles tend to charge when it makes sense — at home overnight, or at a destination where they'll be parked for an extended period anyway.

**Opportunity charging decreases.** Quick top-ups at retail or workplace chargers become less frequent for drivers who know they'll comfortably make it home. The charger at a shopping center is less appealing when you don't really need it.

**Home and destination charging becomes more valuable.** Because long-range drivers charge less frequently, the charging they do prefer is at locations where the car will be parked for a while — home overnight, an all-day work parking spot, an extended destination visit.

## The Overnight Charging Preference Is Strengthening

The longer the EV range, the more fully overnight charging at home or at a residential property satisfies weekly charging needs. A driver who plugs in at 11pm and unplugs at 7am gets 8 hours on a Level 2 charger — that's 120–200 miles of range added per session, more than enough for most drivers' daily use.

For multifamily property owners, this is a direct implication: the demand for residential overnight charging is growing as a percentage of total charging behavior, even as the frequency of individual sessions decreases. More EV drivers want and expect home charging access; they're just doing it less often because modern ranges make it sufficient.

This argues strongly for Level 2 charging at multifamily properties, sized for the number of EV-owning tenants with room to grow. The use case is durable. The economics are straightforward.

## Workplace Charging Remains Strategically Valuable

Even with longer ranges, workplace charging retains a clear value proposition: it's free electricity for employees who charge during a workday, converting idle parking time into a practical benefit. For employers and commercial property owners, it functions as a retention perk and ESG signal.

The behavioral pattern here has shifted slightly. In the early EV era, workplace charging was often necessary — employees with short-range vehicles needed it to make the commute home. With longer-range vehicles, it's a convenience rather than a requirement. That shifts it from a utility to a benefit, which still justifies installation but changes how it's communicated and valued.

## Highway and Fast Charging: Still Critical for Long Trips

Longer everyday ranges don't eliminate the need for DC fast charging — they concentrate it at highway corridors for long-distance trips.

A driver doing their daily commute on battery reserve never needs a fast charger. The same driver on a 400-mile road trip needs reliable DC fast charging every 200–250 miles. As EV ranges extend, the charging behavior segregates more clearly: slow, occasional charging at home and work for daily use; fast, deliberate charging on highway corridors for long trips.

This is exactly the use case NEVI is designed to address — building reliable corridor infrastructure for the long-distance use case that home and workplace charging cannot serve.

## Implications for Infrastructure Decisions

If you're planning EV charging for a property today, understanding how modern charging behavior works helps you size and position the infrastructure correctly:

**At multifamily properties:** Plan for Level 2 installed at resident parking spaces or a dedicated charging area. Sizing should account for the current EV percentage of tenants plus expected growth over the next 3–5 years. Overnight utilization will be the primary use pattern.

**At commercial and office properties:** Level 2 for employee spaces is appropriate. Chargers don't need to be in high-visibility locations since they're used by employees who know they're there, not by drive-in users.

**At retail and high-traffic destinations:** Consider the actual dwell time. Long-format retail with 2–4 hour visits is a reasonable Level 2 application. High-turnover retail benefits more from fast charging if EVs are expected to be a significant percentage of customers.

**At fleet and depot settings:** Analyze your turnaround requirements. Long overnight dwell enables Level 2 for most fleets. Rapid redeployment within hours may require DC fast to meet operational needs.

The EV that comes out of the factory next year will have more range than the one that came out two years ago. Infrastructure decisions made today should account for a user base that increasingly charges less frequently but expects the charging they do to be reliable, convenient, and well-matched to their dwell time.
