# Why EV Charging Pays for Itself in Multifamily Properties

There's a version of EV charging that costs you money. You research hardware, hire a contractor, navigate permits, apply for rebates you may or may not qualify for, manage maintenance calls, and hope the revenue eventually covers what you spent.

Then there's the version Evium offers: you do none of that, and you still make money.

For multifamily property owners, that distinction isn't academic — it changes how you think about EV charging entirely. This isn't a capital expenditure. It's a passive income stream that also differentiates your property in a competitive rental market. Here's why it works.

## The Math Starts Before the First Car Plugs In

Most multifamily owners think about EV charging as a cost center. That framing is wrong, and it's costing them leases.

Under Evium's no-cost model, the company covers 100% of capital and operational costs — hardware, installation, permits, rebates, maintenance, and 24/7 monitoring. The property owner contributes nothing upfront and receives a percentage of charging revenue from day one. There is no break-even calculation because there is no investment to recover.

That's a fundamentally different financial profile than any other amenity upgrade you'd consider. A gym renovation costs capital and generates nothing directly. EV charging, structured correctly, generates revenue with zero outlay.

## Tenant Acquisition: The Real Return You're Not Measuring

The revenue split is visible on a spreadsheet. The lease impact isn't — but it's often larger.

One Evium client reported gaining three new tenants in the first month after installation. That outcome isn't unusual. EV ownership is no longer a niche demographic — it's growing rapidly and concentrating in exactly the renter profile most multifamily owners want: higher income, longer tenure, more likely to renew.

For a prospective tenant with an EV, available charging at home isn't a luxury. It's a requirement. A property without charging isn't on their list, regardless of how the rest of the unit stacks up. By adding EV charging, you're not just upgrading an amenity — you're removing yourself from the rejection pile for a growing segment of qualified renters.

The cost of losing one lease to a competitor who has charging installed is almost certainly higher than any revenue share you'd negotiate. When charging costs you nothing to add, that math is straightforward.

## Competitive Differentiation Is Already Happening

In markets with strong EV adoption — coastal metros, high-income suburban corridors — EV charging has shifted from differentiator to expectation. Properties without it are already at a disadvantage with a meaningful segment of renters.

This isn't speculation about the future. It's the current market in a growing number of ZIP codes, and it's spreading. The window to be the first property in your submarket to offer EV charging — the one that wins leases on the differentiation alone — is closing. The properties acting now are building that advantage; the ones waiting are watching it erode.

## What Zero-Effort Actually Means

The zero-cost model isn't just about money. It's about time and complexity. Evium handles:

- Site assessment and feasibility
- Permit applications and electrical work
- Rebate and incentive processing (federal, state, utility)
- Hardware installation and commissioning
- 24/7 remote monitoring and maintenance
- Revenue tracking and reporting through a real-time owner portal

Property management teams don't absorb new responsibilities. Maintenance staff don't field charger calls. The system operates, generates revenue, and reports back — without creating work.

## What to Expect from Revenue

Revenue per charger depends on utilization — how often tenants charge, session duration, and pricing. In multifamily settings, charging behavior is typically overnight (Level 2), predictable, and grows as EV adoption in the tenant base increases. Properties with more EV-owning tenants generate more revenue, which creates a reinforcing dynamic: charging attracts EV tenants, more EV tenants increase utilization, utilization increases revenue.

Under NEVI projections for DC fast charging, revenue per charger ranges from roughly $900 to $2,100 per month depending on utilization. Level 2 installations in residential settings operate differently — lower per-session cost, higher volume, steadier utilization patterns — but the underlying principle holds. Utilization drives revenue, and multifamily properties are well-positioned for consistent overnight utilization.

## The Opportunity Window

EV charging in multifamily is one of the few property upgrades where acting now is strictly better than acting later. Costs aren't going down. Complexity isn't going away. Competitive pressure is increasing. And the no-cost model means waiting doesn't reduce your financial exposure — it just delays revenue and cedes ground to competitors who moved first.

If your properties don't have EV charging yet, the question isn't whether to add it. The question is how quickly you can get it done.
