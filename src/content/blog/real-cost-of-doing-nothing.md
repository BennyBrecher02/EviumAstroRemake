# The Real Cost of Not Offering EV Charging

Most conversations about EV charging focus on the cost of doing it. The hardware, the installation, the ongoing management. What rarely gets examined is the cost of not doing it — and that number is accumulating quietly on your bottom line right now.

For property owners and operators who've been waiting to see how EV adoption shakes out, this is the article worth reading. Because the market has already moved, and the properties that haven't moved with it are paying a price they may not fully recognize yet.

## Lost Leases: The Hidden Line Item

When a prospective tenant with an EV tours your property and doesn't see charging infrastructure, they don't always tell you that's why they went elsewhere. They just go elsewhere. That means the revenue impact of missing EV charging rarely shows up in your reporting as "lost due to no charging." It shows up as vacancy, as days on market, as concessions made to close leases that wouldn't have required concessions at a comparable property with charging.

EV ownership in the U.S. has grown substantially over the past five years and continues to accelerate. The buyers and renters making decisions today include a growing share of people for whom home charging access is a non-negotiable. A property without it isn't in their consideration set, regardless of how strong the unit or location is.

This is not a future problem. It is a current problem, particularly in high-income suburban markets and coastal metros where EV adoption is already well above national averages — the same markets where competition for quality tenants is most intense.

## Negotiating Position Is Eroding

EV charging has become a point of negotiation in commercial and multifamily leasing. Tenants — both residential and commercial — are increasingly asking for it. In some markets, brokers are fielding inquiries about charging availability as a standard part of property evaluations.

When a competing property across the street offers EV charging and you don't, you've handed them a concrete differentiator they can use in every tour. You've also handed prospective tenants a legitimate reason to push your rent down or negotiate concessions you otherwise wouldn't offer. The amenity gap becomes a pricing gap.

Properties that added EV charging early are now in a stronger negotiating position than those that haven't. That gap is structural and it compounds over time.

## Retrofitting Gets More Expensive the Longer You Wait

Here's what most property owners don't account for when they decide to "wait and see": the cost of doing nothing now is not zero — it shifts costs into the future while simultaneously reducing the value you'd have gotten from acting earlier.

The economics of retrofitting EV charging into a property that was never designed for it are different from adding it during a planned renovation or before a property comes to market. Electrical panel upgrades, trenching, conduit runs — all of these get more expensive when they're not coordinated with other work already happening on-site.

In a no-cost model like Evium's, this isn't your direct capital exposure. But it affects timeline, complexity, and in some cases feasibility. Properties that act while infrastructure upgrades are already in motion — or before electrical capacity becomes a constraint — move faster and more cleanly than those retrofitting later under pressure.

## Regulatory and Market Pressure Is One Direction

The trend lines on EV charging requirements are not ambiguous. California, New York, New Jersey, and a growing list of states have enacted or are enacting requirements that mandate EV-ready infrastructure in new construction and, in some cases, existing buildings. Federal programs like NEVI are channeling billions into charging buildout. Utility programs and state energy commissions are offering rebates and incentives that reduce installation costs — but those programs are not permanent, and their terms change.

Waiting means acting later, at higher cost, potentially under regulatory mandate rather than competitive opportunity, and with fewer incentive dollars available. The window for making EV charging a proactive advantage rather than a compliance obligation is open. It won't stay open indefinitely.

## What Doing Nothing Actually Costs

Add it up:

- Lost leases to properties with charging infrastructure
- Weakened negotiating position with existing and prospective tenants
- Higher future installation costs from retrofitting under pressure
- Missed revenue from a zero-cost income stream that's already generating returns for competitors
- Reduced attractiveness to buyers and investors who evaluate properties on forward-looking amenity completeness

None of these are hypothetical. They're already occurring for properties in competitive markets without EV charging. The question for any property owner is how long they can absorb those costs before the math becomes undeniable.

For most, the answer is: not as long as they think.
