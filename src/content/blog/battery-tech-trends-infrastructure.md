# Battery Technology Trends Every Infrastructure Investor Should Watch

Battery technology has improved steadily over the past decade, and the pace of development is not slowing down. For infrastructure investors and property owners considering long-term EV charging assets, understanding where battery technology is heading is relevant — not to predict the future with precision, but to make decisions that hold up well across a range of scenarios.

Here are the key trends worth tracking and what they mean for the infrastructure decisions in front of you today.

## Solid-State Batteries: More Range, Faster Charging

Solid-state batteries replace the liquid electrolyte in current lithium-ion cells with a solid material. The theoretical benefits are significant: higher energy density (more range per pound), improved safety, longer cycle life, and — most relevant for infrastructure — potentially much faster charging speeds.

Current lithium-ion batteries have charging rate limits driven partly by the liquid electrolyte's behavior under fast charge conditions. Solid-state electrolytes can handle higher charge rates more consistently, which means solid-state EVs may accept charge significantly faster than today's vehicles without the same battery degradation concerns.

Several major automakers and battery manufacturers are targeting commercial solid-state EV production in the late 2020s. Toyota, Samsung SDI, and QuantumScape are among those with active programs.

**Infrastructure implication:** Faster charge acceptance means that DC fast chargers deployed today will remain useful even as batteries improve — vehicles will simply charge faster on the same hardware, up to the charger's power output limit. Deploying higher-power charging infrastructure now isn't overbuilding; it's future-proofing. A 180kW charger that today charges a vehicle in 30 minutes might charge a solid-state vehicle in 15 minutes at the same location.

## V2G and Bidirectional Charging: The Grid as Opportunity

Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) technology allows an EV battery to discharge back into the electrical grid or a building. A parked EV with 300 miles of range is a significant battery — 75–100kWh of stored energy. V2G lets that energy flow back out to help balance grid demand or power a building during peak periods.

Bidirectional charging is already available in select vehicles — the Ford F-150 Lightning, Nissan Leaf (older models), and some Mitsubishi Outlander PHEVs support it. It requires compatible hardware on both the vehicle and the charger sides.

The implications for property owners are notable:

**Revenue diversification.** A bidirectional charger in a multifamily or commercial setting could participate in utility demand response programs — earning credits or payments for discharging stored EV energy during peak demand periods. This is an additional revenue stream on top of session charging revenue.

**Energy resilience.** V2G enables a building to use parked EVs as backup power during grid outages or peak pricing windows, reducing energy costs and improving resilience.

**Regulatory interest is high.** FERC Order 2222 opened wholesale energy markets to aggregated distributed energy resources, including EV batteries. V2G is not theoretical from a grid perspective — the regulatory framework for its commercial use is being built now.

**Infrastructure implication:** Chargers installed today may not be V2G-capable. OCPP-compliant hardware with open communication standards can more readily be integrated with bidirectional management software as it develops. When evaluating charger hardware, asking about bidirectional readiness is a reasonable question for any installation that might still be in service in 2030+.

## Faster Charge Acceptance Is Already Arriving

You don't need to wait for solid-state batteries to see charging speeds improve. Current-generation EVs are already accepting charge at higher rates than their predecessors.

The Hyundai IONIQ 6 can accept 800V charging at up to 240kW. The Porsche Taycan and Audi e-tron GT operate on 800V architecture. Many upcoming models from mainstream automakers are targeting 150–200kW+ charge acceptance. As the vehicle fleet evolves, the average charge acceptance rate of EVs on the road will increase substantially.

This means that DC fast chargers with higher output power levels will see their full capacity utilized by more vehicles over time. A 180kW charger deployed today is limited by the charge acceptance of current vehicles; five years from now, a larger share of the fleet will use that full power output.

**Infrastructure implication:** There's a reasonable argument for deploying higher-power hardware now, particularly at highway and high-traffic locations where faster sessions increase throughput capacity. A location that can serve 4 vehicles per hour at 90kW might serve 8 per hour at 180kW as faster-accepting vehicles become common.

## What Doesn't Change

Amid all the technology evolution, some things remain stable:

The physics of charging at home and work — long dwell times, Level 2 sufficient — don't change with battery improvements. Overnight charging will still work on Level 2 whether the vehicle is 250 or 400 miles of range.

Open standards like OCPP remain relevant regardless of battery chemistry. The communication protocol between charger and network doesn't depend on what's inside the vehicle.

The fundamental economics of zero-cost infrastructure deployment don't change with battery technology. The no-cost model is attractive because it eliminates capital risk, not because it bets on a specific technology trajectory.

## The Takeaway for Infrastructure Decisions

Battery technology evolution is net-positive for charging infrastructure investments. More capable vehicles drive more EV adoption, which drives more utilization and revenue. Faster-charging vehicles increase the throughput potential of existing DC fast infrastructure. V2G creates potential new revenue streams for infrastructure assets already in place.

The risk of deploying infrastructure today isn't obsolescence — it's that the technology works and demand grows faster than you anticipated. That's a good problem to have.

For infrastructure investors, the sensible approach is to deploy OCPP-compliant hardware at appropriate power levels for the use case, built by manufacturers with documented development roadmaps. That foundation holds up across a wide range of technology trajectories.
