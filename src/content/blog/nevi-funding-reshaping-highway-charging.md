# How NEVI Funding Is Reshaping Highway Charging Economics

Federal infrastructure investment doesn't usually translate into direct opportunity for property owners. NEVI is the exception. Understanding what the program does — and how site hosts actually benefit — is worth the time if you operate a fuel station, rest stop, travel plaza, or any high-traffic highway-adjacent property.

## What NEVI Is

The National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure program was created under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, signed in 2021. It allocates $5 billion to states over five years to build out a national network of EV fast charging stations along designated highway corridors.

The goal is a publicly accessible charging station every 50 miles along major highways, with each station providing at least four DC fast charging ports at 150kW or higher. The program targets corridors that connect cities, interstate routes, and underserved rural stretches — the routes where range anxiety is highest and charging infrastructure is thinnest.

Every state received an NEVI allocation. Each state DOT developed its own implementation plan. Funding is now flowing into site selection, installation, and buildout across the country.

## How Site Hosts Benefit

Here's the part most property owners don't know: you don't need to invest to participate.

NEVI funding flows through Charge Point Operators — CPOs — who handle the development, installation, operations, and compliance requirements of each site. Evium operates as a CPO and has already received a NEVI site award in New Mexico.

As a site host, your role is to provide the location. The CPO covers everything else:

- Site development and electrical work
- Hardware procurement and installation
- Network connectivity and software management
- Ongoing maintenance and compliance reporting
- 24/7 monitoring and uptime management

In return, the site host receives a percentage of charging revenue generated at their location. It's a long-term agreement — typically five years with a five-year renewal option — that converts an eligible piece of your property into a passive income stream with no capital outlay.

## The Economics Are Unusual

Highway charging has different economics than multifamily or commercial charging. The relevant metric is throughput — how many vehicles charge per day, and how much revenue each session generates.

DC fast chargers at highway locations are designed for fast turnover: drivers stop, charge to an adequate level for the next leg of their trip, and move on. Session duration is 20–45 minutes, and per-session revenue is higher than Level 2 alternatives. A well-utilized corridor site can generate substantial monthly revenue per charger.

Evium's NEVI projections for a 180kW DC fast charger show revenue ranging from approximately $900/month at low utilization to $2,100/month at high utilization. For a site with four chargers — the NEVI minimum — that's $3,600 to $8,400 per month in gross revenue. The site host receives a negotiated share of that, ongoing, for the duration of the agreement.

## Why Now Is the Window

NEVI buildout is happening in waves. Each state's DOT issues solicitations for eligible sites, evaluates proposals, and awards contracts. Sites that participate in early waves benefit from being part of the initial corridor infrastructure — which drives utilization as drivers rely on the network and plan routes around known charging locations.

Waiting for a later wave means potentially missing the first-mover utilization advantage on a given corridor. It also means the administrative capacity of CPOs is spread thinner as more projects come online simultaneously.

For site hosts who are on or near NEVI-designated corridors, the relevant question is whether you're engaged with a CPO who can move your site through the selection and approval process. Evium's existing NEVI award demonstrates they've completed that process and understand the compliance requirements.

## Compliance and Operations Are Handled

One of the reasons site hosts hesitate about highway charging programs is the perceived operational burden. NEVI compliance requirements are real — there are uptime standards, reporting requirements, pricing transparency mandates, and accessibility specifications.

Under a CPO model, those requirements belong to the CPO, not the site host. The property owner's obligation is to provide the location and maintain access. The CPO is responsible for meeting program standards, maintaining uptime, handling maintenance, and submitting compliance documentation.

For a fuel station or travel plaza operator, this is particularly relevant. Adding EV charging under a CPO model doesn't create a new operational department — it adds a revenue-generating asset that someone else manages.

## The Broader Picture

NEVI is one piece of a larger national buildout. State-level programs, utility-funded infrastructure, and private investment are all flowing into highway and corridor charging simultaneously. The result is a rapidly expanding network that will, within a few years, make long-distance EV travel as reliable as gasoline travel is today.

Site hosts positioned along key corridors now — with infrastructure already installed and operational — will benefit from the utilization growth that comes with network expansion. Every new EV on the road is a potential session at your location. The earlier the infrastructure is in place, the more of that growth you capture.

For eligible sites, there is no meaningful financial argument for waiting.
