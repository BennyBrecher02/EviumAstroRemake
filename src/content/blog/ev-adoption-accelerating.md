# EV Adoption Is Accelerating — What Property Owners Need to Know Now

The EV transition has been described as imminent for so long that it's easy to assume it's still around the corner. It isn't. The shift is happening now, it's accelerating, and the property owners who understand it as a real estate story — not just a technology story — are the ones positioned to benefit.

Here's what the current moment looks like and what it means for your decisions this year.

## The Numbers Have Shifted

EV sales in the U.S. have grown from a fraction of total vehicle sales to a meaningful and growing percentage. Major automakers have committed to substantial EV portfolios. Model availability has expanded dramatically across price points, making EVs accessible to a far broader demographic than the early-adopter market.

The buyer who drove a Tesla in 2018 was a specific profile: tech-forward, high income, early adopter. The EV buyer in 2025 is far more varied — a family buying a Chevy Equinox EV, a business owner choosing a Ford Pro Electric van, a commuter picking up an affordable compact. The market has broadened considerably, and that broadening changes who is looking for EV charging when they shop for an apartment, office space, or retail location.

## Charging Is Becoming an Expectation

The evolution of amenity expectations tends to follow a predictable pattern. A feature starts as a differentiator — the property that has it stands out. Then it becomes standard — the property that doesn't have it stands out, for the wrong reasons.

EV charging is in the middle of that transition right now. In markets with above-average EV adoption — which includes most high-income suburban markets, most coastal metros, and many college towns — charging access has moved from "nice to have" to a decision factor for a meaningful share of prospective tenants and commercial occupants.

Properties that already have charging installed are closing deals on it. Properties without it are answering questions about when it's coming.

## Regulatory Pressure Is Adding Urgency

Beyond market demand, the regulatory environment is pushing in one direction. California's building codes now require EV-ready infrastructure in new multifamily construction. New York and New Jersey have similar requirements expanding over time. At the federal level, the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act created NEVI — a $5 billion program specifically designed to build out national EV charging corridors.

This isn't regulatory speculation. These are enacted laws and funded programs. For property owners, the question is whether you adopt EV infrastructure on your terms, with access to incentive programs and the flexibility to choose your model, or whether you eventually do it under mandate, at higher cost, with fewer options.

The difference between those two paths is real, and it's a function of timing.

## NEVI Is Actively Reshaping the Infrastructure Map

The NEVI program (National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure) is worth understanding specifically, because it illustrates the pace and scale of what's happening.

NEVI allocates federal funds to states for buildout of EV charging along major highway corridors. Site hosts in NEVI-designated locations can partner with a CPO — a Charge Point Operator like Evium — to get charging infrastructure installed at zero cost to the property, with ongoing revenue sharing from day one. Evium has already been awarded a NEVI site in New Mexico and operates as a qualified CPO for the program.

For eligible site hosts, NEVI is essentially a fully funded infrastructure upgrade that generates passive income. The catch is that NEVI designations follow a buildout schedule, and the opportunity to be in a wave of funded installations is time-limited.

## What This Means for Your Portfolio

The practical implication for property owners and operators is straightforward: the properties that will be well-positioned three to five years from now are installing EV charging today.

That doesn't require predicting exactly how fast EV adoption will grow. It requires recognizing that the direction is set, the pace is accelerating, and the cost of acting now — particularly under no-cost models — is zero. The cost of waiting accumulates in competitive disadvantage, lost leases, and higher future installation costs.

Whether you operate multifamily residential, commercial office, retail, parking, or mixed-use assets, the relevant question isn't if EV charging will be expected at your properties. It's when, and whether you'll be ahead of that moment or behind it.

The property owners acting now are building a durable amenity advantage at essentially no cost. The window to do that proactively — rather than reactively — won't stay open indefinitely.
