# OCPP Explained: Why Open Standards Protect Your Charging Investment

EV charging infrastructure is a long-term investment. The hardware you install today may be in service for 10–15 years. The software platform managing it will be upgraded, migrated, or replaced over that time. How those two things relate — hardware and software — is determined in large part by whether your chargers use open communication standards.

OCPP is the standard that matters most. Here's what it is, why it matters, and what it means when a charging provider is OCPP-compliant.

## What OCPP Is

OCPP stands for Open Charge Point Protocol. It's a communication standard that defines how EV charging hardware (the charger) communicates with a back-end management system (the network). Think of it like HTTP for the web — an agreed-upon language that allows different devices and platforms to exchange information reliably.

OCPP was developed by the Open Charge Alliance, a non-profit industry consortium, and is now the de facto global standard for EV charging. Version 1.6 is the most widely deployed; version 2.0.1 adds enhanced features. Evium's platform is OCPP 1.6+ compliant.

OCPP enables functions including:
- Remote start and stop of charging sessions
- Real-time charger status monitoring
- Firmware updates delivered remotely
- Session data reporting and metering
- Load management and power balancing across multiple units
- Access control and authentication
- Fault reporting and diagnostics

Without OCPP, these functions require proprietary software from the same vendor that made the hardware. With OCPP, any compliant charger can communicate with any compliant management platform.

## The Vendor Lock-In Problem

Proprietary charging systems are common. A vendor sells you hardware that only works with their network software. Their platform is the only one that can manage, configure, and report on your chargers. If they raise prices, downgrade service, get acquired, or exit the market, you have limited options:

- Accept the new terms
- Replace the hardware

Neither is a good answer. Hardware replacement is expensive and disruptive. Accepting bad terms is a permanent cost that compounds over the contract life.

This is vendor lock-in, and it's a known risk in the EV charging industry. Early-stage players in particular have come and gone, leaving property owners with stranded hardware that can't be managed without the original vendor's platform.

OCPP eliminates this risk at the protocol level. An OCPP-compliant charger can be connected to any OCPP-compliant management platform. If your current software provider raises prices or shuts down, you migrate your chargers to a different platform without replacing hardware.

## What OCPP Enables in Practice

For Evium clients, OCPP compliance is what makes remote management possible — and remote management is what makes the no-cost, hands-off model work at scale.

**Remote diagnostics.** When a charger experiences a fault, Evium's platform receives the error code via OCPP and can often diagnose and resolve the issue without a site visit. This reduces downtime and keeps utilization — and revenue — from being interrupted by minor technical issues.

**Firmware management.** Charger software can be updated remotely across an entire portfolio. Security patches, feature updates, and protocol upgrades are applied without technicians visiting each site.

**Load management.** For properties with multiple chargers, OCPP enables power sharing across units so the building's electrical capacity isn't overloaded during peak periods. This is particularly important in multifamily settings where many EVs may charge simultaneously overnight.

**Pricing and configuration.** Session pricing, access controls, and charger settings can be updated through the owner portal without physical access to the hardware. That means revenue optimization can happen responsively, without on-site work.

## Why This Matters for Your Specific Investment

If you're installing EV charging under a no-cost model, vendor lock-in may feel like a less urgent concern — the capital isn't yours. But the long-term revenue stream is.

A management platform that underperforms reduces uptime and utilization. A vendor that exits the market leaves your chargers inoperable. OCPP compliance means that in either scenario, the hardware remains functional with a different platform. The asset retains its value and revenue-generating capability regardless of what happens to any single software provider.

For property owners who invest directly in charging infrastructure, this protection is even more significant. The hardware investment is yours. Its long-term functionality should not be contingent on the continued operation of a single software company.

## The Standard That Protects You Long-Term

OCPP 1.6 is not a marketing term. It's a technical specification with real operational implications. Before deploying charging infrastructure at any property — particularly under any model where you own the hardware or have a long-term revenue dependency — verifying OCPP compliance is a meaningful due diligence step.

Evium's platform is built on OCPP because it's the right foundation for infrastructure that needs to remain functional, manageable, and valuable over a 10+ year horizon. It's not a feature. It's a structural protection for the investment, whoever owns it.

Open standards exist precisely to prevent market fragmentation from trapping users in dead-end ecosystems. In EV charging, OCPP is that standard, and its adoption is one of the clearest signals of a provider building for the long term.
